12 Principles of Animation

Whether developing a videogame or filming a movie, animations are always involved and a major component of their success. Animations are critical because they communicate stories and ideas in unique and easy-to-perceive methods that everyone can understand and enjoy. Animations have the capability to connect people throughout the world in techniques not attainable through writing.

The 12 principles of animation were first introduced by Disney imagineers during the 1980s through The Illusion of Life: Disney Animation. They are based on dynamic effects that arise from real world physics. Disney’s animation style has become widely recognized as a “theoretical bedrock” for video production. Animators working on films can capture all the effects desired, but this can be strenuous in games because gameplay systems need control over the animation of interactive elements. Ubisoft adopted and currently use Disney’s Animation techniques to constantly improve and make new games.

SQUASH AND STRETCH

Squash and Stretch captures the behavior of elastic deformation. It is used to set the illusion of gravity, weight, mass and flexibility. For example, a bouncing rubber ball … The ball stretches when it travels up and down and squishes when in contact with the floor. Volume kept consistent: stretch something → thinner, squash something → wider.

.01

ANTICIPATION

Anticipation prepares the viewer for an action about to occur, making the effect more realistic. Ex → Kicking a soccer ball: #1 Swing foot back, #2 steady arms … then kick the ball! Animating movements without anticipation will make the motion quite awkward, stale and lifeless.

.02

STAGING

Staging is very similar to the composition in artwork. The motion is used to guide the viewer's eye and draw attention and keep the focus on what's important within the scene, while keeping the motion of everything else of non-importance to a minimum. Cameras, lighting effects and character composition would assist in the process of honing on what is important.

.03

STRAIGHT AHEAD AND POSE TO POSE

Straight ahead involves drawing frame-by-frame from start to finish. If an animator is striving for fluid, realistic movements, straight ahead action is the best option.

The pose to pose technique is more complicated. One would start out with the beginning frame, the end frame, and a few key frames in-between. Next steps would be to go back and fill in the middle frames. This technique provides more control within the scene and allows to increase the dramatic effect of the motion. Iteration is required and constant changes to attain perfection.

.04

FOLLOW-THROUGH AND OVERLAP ACTION

When a moving object comes to a stop, different parts arrive at a stand still at varying rates due to force of forward momentum. This is where the follow-through and overlapping action occurs. The secondary elements follow through on the primary element, and overlapping its action. Ex. Punch → Head and Torso lead the action → Bent arm dragging behind → Arm snap forward just before impact to deliver blow.

.05

SLOW IN & SLOW OUT

When driving a car, the car does not hit max speed right away. It takes time to accelerate, gaining momentum, and reach a steady speed. Animators call this is called Slow Out. Likewise, braking follows the same scenario, decelerate over a few seconds until at a stand-still. Animators call this Slow In. The ability to control the changing speeds of objects creates an animation that has a superior believability.

.06

ARC

Animation is a mirror of life which is not linear but very dynamic. Majority of life revolves in circular paths called arcs. Arcs are curved trajectories that add the illusion of life to an animated object in action. Without arcs, animation would be stiff and mechanical. Arcs provide natural flow with the rules of gravity.

.07

SECONDARY ACTION

Secondary actions are gestures that support the main action to add more dimension to character animation. They give more personality and insight to what the character is doing or thinking. This includes subtle actions occurring in the scene.

.08

TIME

Timing is about where and when each object is at each frame. Moving an object faster or slower.

.09

EXAGGERATION

Exaggeration is used to make objects more dynamic and to enhance the storytelling effect. It presents a character’s features and actions in an extreme form for comedic or dramatic effect, distortions in facial features, body types, and expressions.

.10

SOLID DRAWING

Solid Drawing is for the sole purpose of making the object 3D and lifelike, anatomy, weight and volume, and lights and shadows.

.11

APPEAL

Appeal is meant to keep the film or game pleasing to the viewer, easy-to-read design, solid drawing, and a personality. There is no right path to appeal other than striving for the balance between detail and simplicity.
