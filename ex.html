For Honor

Now having a deep understanding of what DRL is we can now move to how Ubisoft uses DRL for their games. DReCon trains simulated characters to move in propper Kinematic motion through DRL on their joint actuators (AI muscles) to follow a user controlled animated character. To simplify this, DRL was used in production to give the final product you see when you move your character around in propper Kinematic Motion in Ubisoft’s video games. In production, the DReCon character controller automatically learns to correct any physical problems with the motion of an animated character to track the motion as best as physically possible. This is where DRL is then introduced as these characters are trained in simulated environments where AI DReCon controllers aggressively input random gamepad controls resulting in the system having to learn a strategy to achieve these inputs in propper Kinematic Motion. After all of this is achieved the AI finally uses Motion Matching to generate the most realistic looking animation that is outputted to your screen in gameplay when controlling the character.

Using Ubisoft’s recent game title as an example, For Honor further shows Motion Matching in process as seen above. In the above video you can see how multiple possible animation outcomes are presented from a large dataset of motion-capture animation before the character itself moves. This dataset was acquired using all of the previous deep reinforcement learning on AI and the pieces of animation that violate the constraints the least are continuously combined to generate the resulting character motion on screen as shown.
